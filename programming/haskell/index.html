Haskell

Haskell is a beautiful and amazing language. Everyone would benefit from learning it.

Haskell is so good at preventing programmer mistakes, that it's not unusual for a newly authored program to run correctly the first time. Let that sink in for a minute.

Additionally, Haskell is incredibly expressive. The built in type checker wipes out 90% of the need to declare types. Heck, even writing out the names of formal parameters is often optimized out - look at slices and partial application and points-free function definitions. Ruby boasts about its consicision and power, but it can't hold a candle to the expressive power of Haskell.

My Experience

I've been doing intermittant hobbyist work spanning years, but rarely intensely.

The largest app I wrote was a public transit route finder that used screen-scraped schedules from my home city's transit system.

I've also used it to tear up some Project Euler and Hackerrank.com challenges. It's really not fair to the tests, but there's no points for doing those things in assembly, either.

It's Limits, My Limits

Haskell seems to have a curse of being the programming language of choice for people who only speak in extremely rarified math terms. Their reasoning leaves me quite befuddled at times. This means I have to don battlepants every time I want to grasp some illnamed concept. The first such concept you'll hit will be Monads, but there are tons of them.

Haskell places an enormous syntactic tax on side effects. That is, performing side effects - like drawing a polygon, writing a database, or printing to a file - is an affront to other Haskell code, and it has to be handled with special, pesky syntax and isolated to short depths in the call tree. It's both brilliant and very, very hard to live with. Scala is a mid-flight course correction - one wonders if thye've got the right idea.
